Faster Horses Country Music & Camping Festival Tragedy

2021 Campsite Safety

For Unarmed Event Security Training
Date of Incident: July 16–18, 2021
Location: Michigan International Speedway, Brooklyn, Michigan
Event: Faster Horses Country Music & Camping Festival
Prepared by: James "Fergie" Ferguson
Last Updated: March 29, 2025

Overview

The Faster Horses Country Music & Camping Festival, held July 16–18, 2021, at Michigan International Speedway (MIS) in Brooklyn, Michigan, was marred by the deaths of four attendees in two separate incidents. Three young men—Dawson Brown (20), William "Richie" Mays Jr. (20), and Kole Sova (19)—died from carbon monoxide (CO) poisoning in their camper trailer due to a improperly positioned generator, while a fourth attendee, Melissa Donna Havens (30), was found dead after excessive alcohol consumption. Two other men in the trailer survived but were critically injured. The festival, known as the "party of the summer," drew approximately 40,000 attendees for three days of music and camping, headlined by Luke Combs, Thomas Rhett, and Jason Aldean. These tragedies highlighted significant safety oversights in campsite management, emergency preparedness, and security coordination, offering critical lessons for unarmed event security personnel.

This case study examines the incidents, identifies key failures, and provides practical insights to enhance training and safety protocols for unarmed security teams at similar events.

Background

Event Details: Faster Horses, launched in 2013 by Live Nation, is an annual country music festival at MIS, featuring major acts and extensive camping across 18 campgrounds. The 2021 event was its eighth iteration and Michigan's first major festival post-COVID-19 lockdowns, amplifying attendance and anticipation.

Security Staffing: Over 1,000 personnel were deployed, including unarmed guards from Contemporary Services Corporation (CSC), alongside Michigan State Police (MSP) and Lenawee County Sheriff's Office deputies. Exact numbers of unarmed guards are not specified, but they were tasked with crowd control and campsite monitoring.

Historical Context: The festival had a reputation for rowdy behavior, with prior incidents of assaults and sexual misconduct (91 assaults and 30 sexual misconduct cases reported since 2013). No previous CO-related deaths were documented at Faster Horses, though MIS had a history of such incidents at NASCAR events.

Timeline of Events

  • July 16, 2021 (Day 1): The festival opened with campgrounds accessible from July 15. Performances began, with no major incidents reported initially.
  • July 17, 2021 (Day 2):
    • 7:30 AM: Melissa Donna Havens was found unresponsive at a campsite and pronounced dead. MSP later attributed her death to excessive alcohol consumption (reportedly 3.5 fifths of Fireball whiskey), ruled accidental with contributing obesity complications.
    • 1:30 PM: Emergency responders found five men unresponsive in a travel trailer at a campground near US-12 and Brooklyn Highway. Three (Brown, Mays, Sova) were pronounced dead on-site from suspected CO poisoning; two others (Rayfield Johnson, 20, and Kurtis Stitt, 20) were hospitalized in critical condition. A generator placed too close to the trailer was identified as the source.
  • July 18, 2021 (Day 3): The festival continued without cancellation, closing with Jason Aldean's performance. No further fatalities were reported.
  • Aftermath: Investigations confirmed CO poisoning as the cause of the three deaths, linked to a generator's exhaust blowing into the trailer. A non-functional CO alarm was also noted. Havens' death was unrelated but underscored safety concerns.

Key Failures in Security and Event Management

Inadequate Campsite Safety Training:

  • Unarmed security lacked specific training on campsite hazards like generator placement or CO risks. Most guards received only basic crowd control instruction, unprepared for environmental threats.
  • No evidence of pre-event briefings on CO safety or generator guidelines for staff or attendees.

Poor Campsite Oversight and Design:

  • Campsites, reduced from 1,200 to 800 square feet since 2016, were deemed unsafe by state regulators due to cramped conditions, increasing risks from generators. The trailer's generator was installed incorrectly, with exhaust directed inward—a preventable error if monitored.
  • Security did not enforce or check generator placement, despite known risks at MIS from prior incidents.

Communication and Coordination Gaps:

  • Unarmed guards lacked direct communication tools (e.g., radios) with supervisors or law enforcement, delaying response to the CO incident. Initial discovery relied on a friend, not security patrols.
  • No unified protocol existed between security, medical teams, and organizers to address campsite emergencies swiftly.

Insufficient Staffing for Scale:

  • With 40,000 attendees across a sprawling venue, the guard-to-attendee ratio was inadequate for proactive campsite monitoring. Security was stretched thin, focusing on festival grounds rather than campgrounds.
  • Medical resources (number unspecified) were unprepared for simultaneous mass casualty events.

Failure to Mitigate Known Risks:

  • The festival continued after Havens' death and the CO incident, with no pause to assess safety or inform attendees of risks. Organizers prioritized the event over immediate corrective action.
  • Excessive alcohol consumption, a known issue at Faster Horses, was not addressed through stricter controls or monitoring.

Impact

  • Human Toll: Four deaths (three from CO poisoning, one from alcohol-related causes) and two critical injuries, devastating families and the tight-knit Jackson, Michigan, community.
  • Legal Consequences: Families of the CO victims sued Live Nation and MIS, alleging unsafe camping conditions. A Michigan appeals court upheld a 2022 ruling in October 2024, finding Live Nation not liable, as campsite control rested with attendees. Appeals to the Michigan Supreme Court are pending.
  • Industry Fallout: The tragedy, alongside an MLive investigation revealing 91 assaults and 30 sexual misconduct cases since 2013, tarnished Faster Horses' reputation. The festival canceled its 2025 event, citing plans for a "bigger and better" return.

Lessons for Unarmed Event Security

Comprehensive Hazard Training:

  • Train guards on campsite-specific risks (e.g., CO poisoning, generator safety, alcohol overdose), beyond crowd control. Include recognition of CO symptoms (e.g., dizziness, unconsciousness) and response steps.
  • Conduct venue walkthroughs to identify hazards like cramped campsites or poor ventilation zones.

Proactive Campsite Monitoring:

  • Patrol campgrounds regularly, checking for unsafe generator placement or excessive intoxication. Report issues to supervisors immediately.
  • Educate attendees on generator safety (e.g., placement 20 feet from trailers, exhaust direction) via signage or briefings.
The Faster Horses 2021 Tragedy underscores the critical need for unarmed event security to extend their focus beyond crowd control to campsite safety and emergency readiness. While guards cannot eliminate all risks, their vigilance, training, and coordination can prevent or lessen the impact of such incidents.

Recommendations for Event Organizers

  • Campsite Safety Standards: Mandate larger campsites (e.g., 1,200 square feet) and enforce generator placement rules, providing CO detectors as standard equipment.
  • Staffing Ratios: Increase unarmed security presence in campgrounds, aiming for a 1:50 ratio during peak times, with dedicated hazard monitors.
  • Risk Mitigation: Implement alcohol limits or checkpoints and pause events after fatalities to assess and address safety gaps.
  • Attendee Education: Distribute clear safety guidelines (e.g., generator use, emergency contacts) at entry and via signage.
  • Enhanced Communication Protocols: Equip all guards with radios linked to a central command and emergency services, ensuring rapid reporting of incidents.
  • Emergency Preparedness: Train staff in basic first aid and crowd management to support first responders in mass casualty events.
