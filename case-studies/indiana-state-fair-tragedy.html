Indiana State Fair Stage Collapse

2011 Venue Safety

For Unarmed Event Security Training
Date of Incident: August 13, 2011
Location: Indiana State Fairgrounds, Indianapolis, Indiana
Event: Indiana State Fair
Prepared by: James "Fergie" Ferguson
Last Updated: March 31, 2025

Overview

On August 13, 2011, a catastrophic stage collapse at the Indiana State Fair in Indianapolis killed 7 people and injured 58 out of approximately 12,000 attendees awaiting a performance by the country duo Sugarland. Just before 9:00 PM, a sudden wind gust—estimated at 60–70 mph (97–113 kph)—toppled the temporary stage roof structure during a severe thunderstorm warning. The collapse occurred minutes after fair officials delayed the show but failed to evacuate the grandstand area, despite visible storm clouds and prior weather alerts. The tragedy exposed critical lapses in structural safety, weather monitoring, and emergency decision-making, leading to lawsuits, safety reforms, and a reevaluation of outdoor event protocols.

This case study examines the incident from the perspective of unarmed event security, identifying key failures and providing actionable insights to enhance training and preparedness for environmental and structural emergencies.

Background

Event Details: The Indiana State Fair, an annual agricultural and entertainment event, ran from August 5–21, 2011, at the Indiana State Fairgrounds. The Sugarland concert was part of the Hoosier Lottery Grandstand series, with opening act Sara Bareilles concluding around 8:40 PM.

Security Staffing: Approximately 100–150 unarmed security guards, contracted through private firms like CSC (Contemporary Services Corporation), were deployed alongside Indiana State Police and fair staff. Unarmed guards handled crowd control, entry screening, and perimeter duties.

Historical Context: Temporary stage collapses had occurred elsewhere (e.g., 2009 Big Valley Jamboree, Canada), but the Indiana State Fair had no prior incidents of this scale. The Midwest's summer storm season was a known risk, yet protocols remained lax.

Timeline of Events

Afternoon (August 13, 2011):

  • The National Weather Service (NWS) issued a severe thunderstorm watch at 12:45 PM, escalating to a warning by 8:15 PM, predicting 60 mph winds and hail.
  • Fair officials monitored radar but deemed evacuation unnecessary, expecting the storm to pass north.

8:40–8:49 PM:

  • Sara Bareilles finished her set. A fair announcer delayed Sugarland's start, citing weather, but urged attendees to stay calm without ordering an evacuation.
  • Winds intensified, with visible storm clouds approaching. Security and staff remained at posts, unprepared for immediate action.

8:49 PM:

  • A gust hit the grandstand stage, collapsing the roof rigging onto the crowd. Seven died (four instantly, three later from injuries), including attendees and a stagehand; 58 were injured by debris.

Post-Collapse:

  • Emergency response began within minutes, with police, fire, and medics extracting survivors. The fair canceled events for August 14 but reopened later.
  • Aftermath: Investigations found the stage lacked adequate wind resistance (rated for 40 mph, not 70). Lawsuits settled for $50 million by 2014.

Key Failures in Security and Event Management

Inadequate Training for Weather Emergencies:

  • Unarmed guards received basic crowd control training but no instruction on severe weather protocols, such as recognizing wind hazards or initiating evacuations.
  • No drills addressed structural risks or coordinated responses to sudden storms.

Poor Structural and Weather Oversight:

  • The stage, erected by Mid-America Sound, met minimal code but lacked guy wires or ballast to withstand 70 mph winds, a flaw security did not inspect or report.
  • Fair officials ignored NWS warnings, delaying evacuation decisions until too late.

Communication Gaps:

  • Guards had limited radios, relying on verbal relays to supervisors, delaying real-time weather updates or evacuation calls to police and fair management.
  • No clear chain of command existed between security, state police, and fair officials for weather-related emergencies.

Understaffing for Crisis Response:

  • The guard-to-attendee ratio (1:80–120) was insufficient to manage a rapid evacuation of 12,000 under storm conditions, especially near a single grandstand.
  • Medical teams were unprepared for mass casualties, with security unable to assist effectively.

Delayed Decision-Making:

  • Evacuation was debated but not executed before the collapse, despite 30 minutes of warning. Guards lacked authority or training to push for action.
  • Post-collapse, security struggled to guide panicked attendees away from debris, lacking a rehearsed plan.

Impact

  • Human Toll: 7 deaths (ages 23–60, including Tammy Vandam, Glenn Goodrich, and stagehand Nathan Byrd), 58 injuries (broken bones, concussions), and widespread trauma.
  • Legal Consequences: Over 65 lawsuits against the state, Sugarland, and contractors settled for $50 million by 2014. No criminal charges were filed, but investigations blamed shared negligence.
  • Industry Fallout: Indiana enacted stricter stage safety codes (e.g., 90 mph wind resistance). The tragedy spurred national guidelines for temporary structures (e.g., NFPA 101 updates).

Lessons for Unarmed Event Security

Weather Hazard Training:

  • Train guards to recognize severe weather signs (e.g., dark clouds, sudden wind shifts) and report them immediately, even without formal meteorology skills.
  • Conduct drills for storm-related evacuations, focusing on grandstand or stage areas.

Structural Vigilance:

  • Patrol stage perimeters, reporting visible instability (e.g., swaying rigging, loose supports) to supervisors pre-event, despite not being engineers.
  • Advocate for pre-event safety checks by qualified inspectors if concerns arise.
The most dangerous phrase in event management is "It's never happened before." Weather is not a surprise element—it's a predictable risk factor that requires proactive management and decisive action.

Recommendations for Event Organizers

  • Risk Assessment: Include severe weather scenarios in planning, with mandatory evacuation triggers (e.g., 60 mph wind warnings).
  • Structural Standards: Use stages rated for regional wind maximums (e.g., 90 mph in Midwest), inspected daily by engineers.
  • Staffing Levels: Increase unarmed security near stages (e.g., 1:50 ratio) during high-risk weather, with weather training.
  • Real-Time Monitoring: Assign weather liaisons with radar access, empowering security to act on alerts swiftly.
  • Enhanced Communication Protocols: Equip all guards with radios linked to a central command, ensuring real-time weather alerts and evacuation orders.
  • Emergency Preparedness: Conduct drills for storm-related evacuations, focusing on grandstand or stage areas.
